# PG on Founders — What He Looks for, and Why Vision Fails Without It

> A vision lives or dies inside a particular founder. PG has written more about founder psychology than about ideas — because at YC the founders matter more than the idea (the idea changes; the founders don't).

This document is the lens for any vision question that asks "is this the right founder for this thing?" — and PG's answer is more textured than the standard "domain expertise" reply.

---

## The five qualities (from _What We Look for in Founders_, 2010)

PG's actual list, in his order:

### 1. Determination

> "This has turned out to be the most important quality in startup founders. We thought when we started Y Combinator that the most important quality would be intelligence. That's the myth in the Valley. And certainly you don't want founders to be stupid. But as long as you're over a certain threshold of intelligence, what matters most is determination."

PG decomposes determination further in _The Anatomy of Determination_ (2009): determination = **willfulness × discipline**, aimed by **ambition**. Willfulness without discipline gives you addiction or wing flutter. Discipline without willfulness gives you a competent middle manager. Both, aimed at something worth doing, give you a founder.

**The diagnostic for vision work:** Does the founder still seem fascinated by this problem when you stress-test it for an hour? Determination shows as not-getting-bored.

### 2. Flexibility

> "You do not however want the sort of determination implied by phrases like 'don't give up on your dreams.' The world of startups is so unpredictable that you need to be able to modify your dreams on the fly."

PG's metaphor: a running back. Determined to get downfield, but at any given moment may go sideways or backwards to get there. The founder who insists the vision must come true _exactly_ as drafted on day one is dangerous, not admirable.

**Tied tightly to:** _The Right Kind of Stubborn_ (2024) — the persistent are attached to the goal; the obstinate are attached to their first ideas about how to reach it. "The persistent are like boats whose engines can't be throttled back. The obstinate are like boats whose rudders can't be turned."

**The diagnostic:** When you point out a problem in the vision, does the founder lean in (the Collison brothers' "predatory intensity") or do their eyes glaze over? The first is persistence. The second is obstinacy, which is "a form of not giving up that's easily practiced by the stupid."

### 3. Imagination

> "Intelligence does matter a lot of course. It seems like the type that matters most is imagination. It's not so important to be able to solve predefined problems quickly as to be able to come up with surprising new ideas. In the startup world, most good ideas seem bad initially. If they were obviously good, someone would already be doing them."

The Airbnb story: PG funded them not because the idea seemed good — it seemed _too crazy_ — but because the founders had been selling Obama/McCain breakfast cereal to fund the company. That signaled imagination of the right naughtiness.

**The diagnostic:** Does the vision have at least one element that sounds slightly crazy to a generic professional adult? If everything sounds reasonable, the founder hasn't imagined enough.

### 4. Naughtiness

> "Though the most successful founders are usually good people, they tend to have a piratical gleam in their eye. They're not Goody Two-Shoes type good. Morally, they care about getting the big questions right, but not about observing proprieties... They delight in breaking rules, but not rules that matter."

This is the quality YC tests for via Sam Altman's question: _tell us about a time when you hacked something to your advantage_ (where "hacked" means beating a system, not breaking a computer).

**The diagnostic:** Has this founder ever circumvented something? Bent a rule that didn't matter to get to a result that did? A founder who has only ever played inside the lines is at a structural disadvantage.

### 5. Friendship

> "Empirically it seems to be hard to start a startup with just one founder. Most of the big successes have two or three. And the relationship between the founders has to be strong."

PG's metaphor: "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be."

**The diagnostic:** If the vision describes "I", probe for the cofounder. If the cofounder relationship is "we met at a meetup three months ago", that's a red flag PG would name. PG's _18 Mistakes_ essay lists "Single Founder" as mistake #1 and "Fights Between Founders" as #17.

## Why-this-founder — what to listen for

The conventional VC framing of "founder-market fit" is about credentials. PG's framing is sharper and more about _prepared mind_ than CV:

- Did the founder _live_ the problem before they decided to solve it?
- Are they _at the leading edge_ of the field where this problem is forming?
- Would they still work on this problem if no one funded it?
- Are they the _user_ of the thing they're building, not just the seller?

From _Organic Startup Ideas_:

> "If I were doing Viaweb again, I'd open our own online store. If we had, we'd have understood users a lot better. I'd encourage anyone starting a startup to become one of its users, however unnatural it seems."

**The diagnostic question:** _If we paused this conversation and asked the founder to describe their own bad day with the current state of this problem, can they?_ If yes, they're prepared. If they describe somebody else's bad day, they're guessing.

## Founder mode — relevant to the vision's 5-year picture

From _Founder Mode_ (2024):

> "The conventional wisdom about how to run larger companies is mistaken... what they were being told was how to run a company you hadn't founded — how to run a company if you're merely a professional manager. But this m.o. is so much less effective that to founders it feels broken."

For vision work, this matters when you ask the founder _what does the world look like in five years_. If their five-year vision is "I'll have hired a great team and delegated and stepped back", that's manager mode, which Brian Chesky's Airbnb tried and described as disastrous. PG's prediction is the opposite: the founder stays involved in the details, runs skip-level meetings, treats the company as something they're still shaping — not something they're handing to a CEO.

**The diagnostic:** Is the founder's 5-year vision built around _them still being in it_, or built around _them having exited_? PG's framing says: the great founders are still founding at year 10.

## The 18 mistakes — relevant to stress-testing the founder, not just the idea

From _The 18 Mistakes That Kill Startups_ (2006). The mistakes that show up at the vision stage:

| #   | Mistake                             | What it looks like in a vision                                            |
| --- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Single Founder                      | One name on the doc, no cofounder relationship described                  |
| 3   | Marginal Niche                      | "We're avoiding competition by going after X tiny segment"                |
| 4   | Derivative Idea                     | "It's like X but better" — no original insight                            |
| 5   | Obstinacy                           | The founder describes the vision as a fixed plan rather than a hypothesis |
| 10  | No Specific User in Mind            | The customer is "small businesses" or "consumers"                         |
| 16  | Not Wanting to Get Your Hands Dirty | Vision describes only elegant parts, no schleps                           |
| 18  | A Half-Hearted Effort               | The founder still has a day job and intends to keep it                    |

These aren't dimensions to score formally — they're tells to listen for during the Socratic interview.

## The condensed founder filter

When you read a vision draft and assess the founder behind it, you're asking these questions in order:

1. **Have they lived this problem?** (Organic origin, not "researched market opportunity")
2. **Can they build it themselves?** (Or are they trying to commission their vision?)
3. **Are they the right kind of stubborn?** (Attached to the goal, not the first plan)
4. **Are they at the leading edge?** (Living in the future of this field)
5. **Do they have a cofounder, and is it real?** (Friendship that survives a sock-pulling dog)
6. **Have they shown naughtiness somewhere?** (Hacked a system, not always played inside the lines)
7. **Would they still do this if nobody funded it?** (Obsession vs. ambition-shopping)

A vision that passes filter 1 from _pg-on-ideas.md_ but fails most of these is a real problem on a wrong founder. That's fixable — but only by changing founders or by the founder honestly closing the gap.
