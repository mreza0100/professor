# PG on Ambition — Frighteningly Big Ideas, Blurry Visions, Heading West

> A vision should be ambitious enough to be worth a decade of life, but PG's view of ambition is unusual: the best ambitious ideas are **terrifying**, the best visions are **blurry**, and the way to get to something big is to start with something deceptively small.

This document is the lens for evaluating the _scale_ of a vision — and the trap of pretending to know more about the future than is possible to know.

---

## Frighteningly ambitious ideas (2012)

> "One of the more surprising things I've noticed while working on Y Combinator is how frightening the most ambitious startup ideas are... The biggest startup ideas are terrifying. And not just because they'd be a lot of work. The biggest ideas seem to threaten your identity: you wonder if you'd have enough ambition to carry them through."

> "It means these ideas are invisible to most people who try to think of startup ideas, because their subconscious filters them out. Even the most ambitious people are probably best off approaching them obliquely."

**The diagnostic for vision work:** If the founder's 5-year picture feels comfortable, sensible, and within their existing imagined competence — it's probably not ambitious enough. The truly big visions cause the founder to wonder _whether they're the person who can pull this off_. That discomfort is signal, not noise.

PG's own examples in the essay: a new search engine, replacing email, replacing universities, internet drama, the next Steve Jobs, bringing back Moore's Law, ongoing automatic medical diagnosis. Each one sounds preposterous in one sentence — which is precisely why each one is undervalued.

---

## The blurry vision — better than the clear one

This is one of the most important and most-missed PG ideas. From the same essay:

> "I think the way to use these big ideas is not to try to identify a precise point in the future and then ask yourself how to get from here to there, like the popular image of a visionary. You'll be better off if you operate like Columbus and just head in a general westerly direction. Don't try to construct the future like a building, because your current blueprint is almost certainly mistaken. Start with something you know works, and when you expand, expand westward."

> "**The popular image of the visionary is someone with a clear view of the future, but empirically it may be better to have a blurry one.**"

**This matters for the vision factory:** A common request is "help me articulate a precise 5-year vision." PG's view is that a _too-precise_ vision is a structural mistake — it tells you the founder is over-fitting on a future they can't actually see.

**The diagnostic:** Does the founder's 5-year picture have the texture of a _direction_ (with optional flexibility about exact endpoints) or the texture of a _blueprint_ (this product, this market, this revenue)? The former is healthy. The latter is fragile.

**How to write a vision that respects this:** State the direction in vivid, definite terms (Thiel's "definite optimism"), but acknowledge inside the doc that the specific path will be discovered, not designed. _"We're headed west. We don't know if the new world is India or America. But we know we're going."_

## Start with something deceptively small

> "Empirically, the way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things. Want to dominate microcomputer software? Start by writing a Basic interpreter for a machine with a few thousand users. Want to make the universal web site? Start by building a site for Harvard undergrads to stalk one another."

> "Don't say, for example, that you're going to replace email. If you do that you raise too many expectations. Your employees and investors will constantly be asking 'are we there yet?' and you'll have an army of haters waiting to see you fail. Just say you're building todo-list software. That sounds harmless."

**The diagnostic:** Does the vision _open_ with the frighteningly-ambitious framing, or with the deceptively-small thing? PG says it should open with the small thing, even though the founders may secretly know the big thing.

**Tactical corollary:** If you're writing the vision doc, write two layers — the surface deceptive-small wedge (what to tell most people) and the internal frighteningly-ambitious north star (what the founders themselves believe). Both are real. They serve different audiences.

## Ambition is malleable — most people have too little

From _The Anatomy of Determination_:

> "Most people don't know how ambitious to be, especially when they're young. They don't know what's hard, or what they're capable of. And this problem is exacerbated by having few peers. Ambitious people are rare, so if everyone is mixed together randomly, as they tend to be early in people's lives, then the ambitious ones won't have many ambitious peers... almost everyone is practically malnourished when it comes to ambition."

**The diagnostic for vision work:** When the founder describes the 5-year picture, is your honest reaction "this is more ambitious than the median pitch I see" or "this is what every YC application looks like"? Most visions are under-ambitious because the founder has been surrounded by under-ambitious peers. The job of a sharp interviewer is to push the ambition larger _without_ pushing it into fantasy.

A good question: _"What's the version of this vision that you've been afraid to put on paper because you weren't sure you could carry it?"_

## Live in the future, then build what's missing — at scale

The blue-collar version of vision-setting from PG's perspective is: _don't try to predict the future. Live there. Then describe what's still missing from where you're standing._

> "Once you're living in the future in some respect, the way to notice startup ideas is to look for things that seem to be missing. If you're really at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field, there will be things that are obviously missing. What won't be obvious is that they're startup ideas."

For _vision_ (as opposed to _idea_), the equivalent question is: _if you assume this product wins, what does the world look like? Not from a press-release perspective — from a "what's the new normal that people take for granted" perspective._

PG's standard for a good north-star vision is _something people in 50 years will look back at and find barbaric that we ever lived without_. From _Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas_:

> "It will seem preposterous to future generations that we wait till patients have physical symptoms to be diagnosed with cancer... people in the future will feel as sorry for us as we do for the generations that lived before anaesthesia and antibiotics."

That's the bar. A north star vision should be the thing that the future will find barbaric we didn't have.

## What this means for the vision factory

When you assess the _ambition_ of a vision draft, run these filters:

| Filter                | Pass signal                                          | Fail signal                                              |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Frighten test         | Founder is slightly uncertain they can pull it off   | Founder is fully comfortable with the plan               |
| Blurriness            | 5-year picture is direction + texture, not blueprint | Specific products, specific revenue, specific milestones |
| Deceptive-small wedge | Today's product is a small, harmless-sounding thing  | Day-one launch is the giant ambition itself              |
| Anti-ambition-poverty | Vision is more ambitious than the median pitch       | Vision is "we'll be the leading platform for..."         |
| Barbaric-in-50-years  | Future generations would feel sorry for us           | Future generations would barely notice the difference    |

A vision that fails the "frighten test" but passes the "specific business model" test is over-engineered. PG's view: better the other way around.
