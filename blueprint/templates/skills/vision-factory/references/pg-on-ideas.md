# PG on Ideas — How to Tell a Real Vision from a Sitcom One

> Source: Paul Graham's essays. The skill draws from these directly. When the founder hands you a vision, this is the lens you use to read it.

The single deepest mistake a founder can make at the vision stage is to _think up_ a vision instead of _noticing_ one. Most failure starts here. Everything else in this document is a corollary.

---

## The three qualities of a real startup idea

Quoting PG directly (_How to Get Startup Ideas_, 2012):

> "The very best startup ideas tend to have three things in common: they're something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing."

When you grill a founder on their vision, you are looking for all three. Missing any one is a structural problem, not a stylistic one.

1. **Founders want it themselves** — not "we believe there's a market for X" but "we use this every week / we ourselves were the customer." The Buchheit test: _would you use this thing yourself, if you hadn't written it?_ PG: "you'd be surprised how often the answer is no."
2. **Founders can build it** — domain expertise, technical depth, the leading edge of the relevant field. If they can't build it, they're going to commission it from someone who doesn't share their vision.
3. **Few realize it's worth doing** — if it's obvious to everyone, the well-funded incumbents are already on it. The most valuable ideas look slightly stupid from the outside.

---

## Made-up / "sitcom" ideas — the dominant failure mode

Quoting PG:

> "Why do so many founders build things no one wants? Because they begin by trying to think of startup ideas. That m.o. is doubly dangerous: it doesn't merely yield few good ideas; it yields bad ideas that sound plausible enough to fool you into working on them."

> "At YC we call these 'made-up' or 'sitcom' startup ideas. Imagine one of the characters on a TV show was starting a startup. The writers would have to invent something for it to do."

**The tell:** When the founder runs the idea by friends, friends don't say "I would never use this." They say "Yeah, maybe I could see using something like that." That reaction sums across the population to zero users.

**Push-back rule:** If the vision describes the customer in third person ("users", "the market", "small businesses"), it's probably made-up. Real ideas have a specific name attached — usually the founder's own.

## The well — depth beats breadth

PG's geometry: imagine a graph where x is "how many people might want this" and y is "how much they want it" (inverted so companies are _holes_).

> "You can either dig a hole that's broad but shallow, or one that's narrow and deep, like a well. Made-up startup ideas are usually of the first type. Lots of people are mildly interested in a social network for pet owners. Nearly all good startup ideas are of the second type."

**The diagnostic question PG actually asks:**

> "Who wants this right now? Who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of? If you can't answer that, the idea is probably bad."

If the founder's vision can't answer this — by name, with a real person they could call — it's not a well. It's a shallow hole.

**The well also needs a path out.** Facebook started with Harvard but colleges were similar enough to spread to. Stripe started with YC companies. Microsoft started with Altair owners. A pure niche with no expansion vector is a lifestyle business, not a startup.

## Organic vs. made-up — and why "noticing" is the verb

> "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.' At YC we call ideas that grow naturally out of the founders' own experiences 'organic' startup ideas. The most successful startups almost all begin this way."

Organic ideas come from _living in the future_. Paul Buchheit's phrase, which PG quotes: "people at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field 'live in the future.' Combine that with Pirsig and you get: **Live in the future, then build what's missing.**"

**The pattern:** External stimulus hits a prepared mind. Drew Houston forgets his USB stick. Bill Gates hears about the Altair. Mark Zuckerberg already lives online. The stimulus only produces a company because the founder's prior experience let them _see_ the opportunity.

**The diagnostic:** Ask the founder how they came to this idea. If the answer is "I sat down to think of startup ideas and this one seemed good", that's a red flag. If the answer is "I kept running into this problem in my work / life / hobby and eventually I built something for myself", that's the right shape.

## Schlep blindness — the unsexy idea sitting in plain sight

From _Schlep Blindness_ (2012):

> "Schlep was originally a Yiddish word but has passed into general use in the US. It means a tedious, unpleasant task... Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness."

PG's canonical example is Stripe — every hacker for a decade knew how painful payments were, but they all built recipe sites instead because their unconscious shrank from "deals with banks, fraud, regulations." Stripe got smooth sailing on user acquisition _because_ schlep blindness kept competitors away.

> "That scariness makes ambitious ideas doubly valuable. In addition to their intrinsic value, they're like undervalued stocks in the sense that there's less demand for them among founders."

**The diagnostic trick PG recommends:**

> "Take yourself out of the picture. Instead of asking 'what problem should I solve?' ask **'what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?'**"

If the founder's vision describes work that _no one would want to do_, that's often a good sign, not a bad one. If it describes only the elegant parts ("we write some clever code, push it to a server, watch money roll in"), be suspicious.

## Toys, not products — why ideas dismissed as toys are often good

> "When something is described as a toy, that means it has everything an idea needs except being important. It's cool; users love it; it just doesn't matter. But if you're living in the future and you build something cool that users love, it may matter more than outsiders think."

Microcomputers, BackRub, The Facebook — all dismissed as toys at first. PG: "At YC we're excited when we meet startups working on things that we could imagine know-it-alls on forums dismissing as toys. To us that's positive evidence an idea is good."

**The diagnostic:** Does the vision describe something a serious VC would dismiss as unserious? That's often a feature.

## The "crowded market" thesis

If a founder's pitch tries to claim "nobody else is doing this", PG's view is the opposite:

> "A crowded market is actually a good sign, because it means both that there's demand and that none of the existing solutions are good enough. A startup can't hope to enter a market that's obviously big and yet in which they have no competitors."

> "You don't need to worry about entering a 'crowded market' so long as you have a thesis about what everyone else in it is overlooking. In fact that's a very promising starting point... You have to be able to phrase it in terms of something the incumbents are overlooking. Best of all is when you can say that they didn't have the courage of their convictions, and that your plan is what they'd have done if they'd followed through on their own insights."

**The diagnostic:** Make the founder name the precise thing the incumbents are overlooking — and why they're overlooking it (lock-in, ego, cannibalization, schlep blindness, can't see it from where they sit). "We have a better UX" is not a thesis. "Google's incentives now reward cluttered results over fast ones" _is_ a thesis.

---

## "Why now?" — PG's view (which is different from Sequoia's)

PG doesn't really frame this as "why now". He frames it as: _are you at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field?_ If yes, you don't have to ask why now — you ARE the why now. You can see the gaps because you're standing where the gaps are forming.

> "If you're at the leading edge of some rapidly changing field, you don't have to look for waves; you are the wave."

**The diagnostic:** If the founder's "why now" thesis is a list of macro trends (AI, GDPR, COVID), that's pasted-on. If their "why now" is "I've been doing X for years and three things just shifted that I can feel in my work every day", that's organic.

---

## What this means for the vision factory

When you read a vision draft, run these filters:

| Filter                | Pass signal                                   | Fail signal                               |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Three qualities       | Founders want it + can build it + obscure     | Any one missing                           |
| Sitcom test           | Specific named user with urgent pain          | "Users", "small businesses", "the market" |
| The well              | Crappy v1 would still get adopted             | Plausible but tepid demand                |
| Buchheit test         | "Yes, I'd use this even if I hadn't built it" | "Well, _people_ would use it"             |
| Organic origin        | External stimulus hit a prepared mind         | Brainstormed in a coffee shop             |
| Schlep                | Involves work others avoid                    | Pure elegant-code play                    |
| Toy-grade             | Sounds slightly unserious                     | Sounds like a corporate strategy slide    |
| Crowded-market thesis | Names what incumbents are overlooking AND why | "Nobody else does X"                      |
| Living in the future  | Founder is at the edge of the relevant field  | Pasted-on macro-trend thesis              |

A vision that fails most of these is not a vision yet. It's a hypothesis the founder hasn't tested against themselves.
