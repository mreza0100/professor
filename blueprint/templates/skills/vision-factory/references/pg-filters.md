# PG Filters — Consolidated Quick Reference

> One-stop reference of every filter from the other reference docs, organized by what you'd ask of a vision. Use this when scoring (Mode C) and when you need a fast cross-check.

This document deliberately repeats material from `pg-on-ideas.md`, `pg-on-founders.md`, `pg-on-ambition.md`, and `pg-on-survival.md`. The other docs explain _why_. This doc is the _checklist_.

---

## The ten filters

When you read a vision, run these ten in order. They are scored PASS / CONDITIONAL / FAIL.

### 1. ORGANIC — Did this idea come from the founder's lived experience?

- **PASS:** Founder describes a specific moment where they ran into the problem in their own work/life. External stimulus hit a prepared mind. They are (or were) the user.
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder has domain expertise but heard about the problem from others. They've watched it but haven't lived it.
- **FAIL:** "We brainstormed in a coffee shop." Founder is selling a problem they don't personally feel.

PG: _"The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'"_

### 2. WELL — Does someone want this so urgently they'd use a crappy v1?

- **PASS:** Named users (real or tightly-described) who would adopt today even with a half-built product. The narrowness is a byproduct of depth.
- **CONDITIONAL:** Demand exists but isn't urgent. Users would say "yeah, maybe I'd try that."
- **FAIL:** Broad shallow demand. Lots of people mildly interested. Pet-owner-social-network shape.

PG: _"Who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of?"_

### 3. BUCHHEIT — Would the founder use this if they hadn't built it?

- **PASS:** Yes, immediately, and they have evidence (they were using a worse version of it before they built this).
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder _would_ use it but it's not central to their week.
- **FAIL:** Founder doesn't use it, doesn't need it, is building it because "the market" needs it.

PG: _"You'd be surprised how often the answer is no."_

### 4. SCHLEP — Does this involve work that scares off competitors?

- **PASS:** The product involves real schleps (regulation, integrations, sales, slow trust-building) that the founder has consciously decided to take on. Their schlep blindness is overcome.
- **CONDITIONAL:** The product has some schleps but the founder is hoping to avoid the hardest ones.
- **FAIL:** "Pure elegant code, deploy, watch revenue." No schlep means easy entry for everyone else.

PG: _"A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake."_

### 5. CROWDED-MARKET THESIS — What are incumbents overlooking, and why?

- **PASS:** Founder names the specific blind spot incumbents have AND explains the structural reason they have it (lock-in, ego, cannibalization, schlep blindness, can't see from where they sit).
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder names the gap but the "why" is weak ("they're just bad at it").
- **FAIL:** "Nobody else is doing this" OR "we have better UX."

PG: _"Best of all is when you can say that they didn't have the courage of their convictions, and that your plan is what they'd have done if they'd followed through on their own insights."_

### 6. LIVING-IN-THE-FUTURE — Is the founder at the leading edge?

- **PASS:** Founder is demonstrably at the edge of the relevant rapidly-changing field. Their "why now" is "this is what my work feels like every day."
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder has been adjacent to the edge but not at it. Their "why now" is partly theoretical.
- **FAIL:** "Why now" is a list of macro trends (AI, GDPR, COVID) pasted onto an unrelated idea.

PG: _"If you're at the leading edge of some rapidly changing field, you don't have to look for waves; you are the wave."_

### 7. RIGHT-KIND-OF-STUBBORN — Persistent or obstinate?

- **PASS:** Founder leans in when you push back. Attached to the goal, not the plan. Hears criticism with "almost predatory intensity" (PG on the Collisons).
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder is determined but defensive in places. Plan-attached on some details that should be flexible.
- **FAIL:** Founder's eyes glaze over when you point out a problem. Defends the original plan against evidence.

PG: _"The persistent are like boats whose engines can't be throttled back. The obstinate are like boats whose rudders can't be turned."_

### 8. AMBITION — Is the vision frighteningly big AND blurry?

- **PASS:** The 5-year picture is large enough that the founder is slightly uncertain they can pull it off. The path is described as a direction (heading west), not a blueprint (this product, this market, this revenue).
- **CONDITIONAL:** Either too small (founder fully comfortable) OR too rigidly specified (claims to know exactly which products will exist in year five).
- **FAIL:** "We'll be a $10M ARR profitable company" or "We'll be the leading platform for X." Either too modest or too clichéd to count.

PG: _"The popular image of the visionary is someone with a clear view of the future, but empirically it may be better to have a blurry one."_

### 9. SURVIVAL — Is there a credible path to default-alive?

- **PASS:** Founder knows whether they'll be default-alive or default-dead at each milestone. Path to ramen profitability or to growth-rate-that-justifies-investment is named.
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder has a plan but it assumes a smooth fundraising pipeline.
- **FAIL:** "We'll raise more money before we run out." Implicit infinite-runway assumption.

PG: _"Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead."_

### 10. TOP-IDEA-IN-MIND — Is this problem what the founder thinks about in the shower?

- **PASS:** Yes, demonstrably. Founder cannot stop talking about this problem. It's their top idea.
- **CONDITIONAL:** Founder is engaged but the top idea is fundraising, or hiring, or some legacy dispute.
- **FAIL:** This problem is one of three things the founder is "exploring." They're idea-shopping.

PG: _"It's hard to do a really good job on anything you don't think about in the shower."_

## Final verdict

| Outcome    | Threshold                                                                                                      |
| ---------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **GREEN**  | 8+ PASS, 0 FAIL on filters 1-5 (the ideas filters). Vision is fundable. Go build.                              |
| **YELLOW** | 5-7 PASS, or any CONDITIONAL on filters 1-3. Vision needs sharpening but the core is sound.                    |
| **RED**    | Fewer than 5 PASS, or any FAIL on filters 1-3. The vision has a structural problem. Rethink before continuing. |

Filters 1-3 (ORGANIC, WELL, BUCHHEIT) are **load-bearing**. A FAIL on any one of these means the vision doesn't yet exist as a real thing — it's a hypothesis the founder hasn't actually tested against themselves. Everything else is fixable. These three are not.

## What's deliberately NOT in this rubric

This is intentional. Most VC rubrics include these. PG's framework largely doesn't:

- **Market size (TAM)** — PG doesn't care about TAM at the seed stage. He cares about whether _anyone_ wants the thing urgently. Path-out matters more than starting size.
- **Business model / unit economics** — useful later; at vision stage it's premature. "Make something people want" comes first.
- **Moat** — PG's view: don't worry about competitors at vision stage. Focus on users. Moats emerge from execution, not from vision-stage strategy.
- **Regulatory** — relevant to specific industries but not a universal vision-stage question.
- **Team beyond founders** — relevant later; at vision stage, the founders are the team.

If a particular vision genuinely needs one of these dimensions (e.g., a fintech vision that's regulatory-bound), add it as a domain note. But don't pretend they belong in the core PG-grounded rubric. They don't.
