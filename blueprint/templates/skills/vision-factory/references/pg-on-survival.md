# PG on Survival — Growth, Default Alive, Doing Things That Don't Scale

> A vision that can't survive contact with reality isn't a vision. PG's writing on growth, ramen profitability, and unscalable early-stage work is where vision meets the founder's actual week-to-week life.

This document is the lens for the _execution_ dimensions of the vision — the parts that test whether the founder has a path from today to the 5-year picture, not just the picture itself.

---

## Startup = Growth

From _Startup = Growth_ (2012):

> "A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary for a startup to work on technology, or take venture funding, or have some sort of 'exit.' The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth."

> "Startups are so hard that you can't be pointed off to the side and hope to succeed. You have to know that growth is what you're after. The good news is, if you get growth, everything else tends to fall into place. Which means you can use growth like a compass to make almost every decision you face."

PG's quantitative bar: weekly growth rate. 10% a week is great, 7% is good, 5% is OK, 1% is dying. (Different essay, _Startup = Growth_.) The point isn't the exact number — it's that the founder has a number, knows where they are on it, and orients decisions around it.

**The diagnostic for vision work:** Does the vision treat growth as the _compass_ or as one of many metrics? Founders who list ten KPIs in their vision usually don't have one. PG's view: you need one. Use it to decide everything else.

**For redwoods vs bean sprouts:** Not every newly-founded company is a startup. If the vision is for a profitable lifestyle business — that's fine, but call it that. The vision factory protocol is built for redwoods, not bean sprouts. A founder who says "I want a $5M ARR profitable consulting business" doesn't need this skill. A founder who says "I want this to become the thing every X uses" does.

## Default Alive or Default Dead

From _Default Alive or Default Dead?_ (2015):

> "When I talk to a startup that's been operating for more than 8 or 9 months, the first thing I want to know is almost always the same. Assuming their expenses remain constant and their revenue growth is what it has been over the last several months, do they make it to profitability on the money they have left? Or to put it more dramatically, **by default do they live or die?**"

> "The startling thing is how often the founders themselves don't know."

PG's framing: founders default-dead but who _assume investors will save them_ are in the **fatal pinch** — "default dead + slow growth + not enough time to fix it."

**For vision work:** When the founder describes the 5-year picture, ask: _what's your default trajectory if no new money comes in?_ If the vision has no answer — if it implicitly assumes a perpetual fundraising pipeline — that's a structural vulnerability the vision doc should name.

**PG's recommendation:** "Instead of thinking of the future with vague optimism, explicitly separate the components. Say 'We're default dead, but we're counting on investors to save us.' Maybe as you say that, it will set off the same alarms in your head that it does in mine."

## Ramen profitability

From _Ramen Profitable_ and _Startups in 13 Sentences_:

> "Ramen profitable" means a startup makes just enough to pay the founders' living expenses. It's not rapid prototyping for business models (though it can be), but more a way of hacking the investment process. Once you cross over into ramen profitable, it completely changes your relationship with investors. It's also great for morale."

**For vision work:** Is there a credible path to ramen profitability described — even vaguely — in the founder's near-term picture? A vision that requires $50M of capital just to get to a usable product is fragile. A vision that gets to ramen profitability with two people and a credit card before raising serious money is anti-fragile.

This isn't a rule. Capital-intensive ideas (hardware, biotech) need real money. But for software, asking "could you ramen-profit your way to product/market fit?" is a useful filter.

## Do things that don't scale

From _Do Things that Don't Scale_ (2013):

> "One of the most common types of advice we give at Y Combinator is to do things that don't scale. A lot of would-be founders believe that startups either take off or don't. You build something, make it available, and if you've made a better mousetrap, people beat a path to your door as promised. Or they don't, in which case the market must not exist. Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off."

The Collison installation: "When anyone agreed to try Stripe they'd say 'Right then, give me your laptop' and set them up on the spot." Airbnb founders going door-to-door in New York to take professional photos of hosts' apartments.

**For vision work:** Does the founder's near-term picture (the first 6-18 months) include manual, embarrassing, unscalable work? If the picture is "we launch on Product Hunt, the algorithm picks us up, we hit 10K users in week one" — that's a fantasy of frictionless growth. PG: "startups take off because the founders make them take off."

**Diagnostic question:** _What's the most embarrassing, manual, unscalable thing you're willing to do for your first 100 users?_ If the founder can't name something, they haven't internalized what early-stage actually feels like.

## Insanely great — the standard for early product

PG quotes Steve Jobs:

> "All the most successful startups we've funded have, and that probably doesn't surprise would-be founders. What novice founders don't get is what insanely great translates to in a larval startup. When Steve Jobs started using that phrase, Apple was already an established company. He meant the Mac (and its documentation and even packaging — such is the nature of obsession) should be insanely great. But that's a different problem from a larval startup, where the question is how to get insane greatness _into_ the seed."

> "The way founders solve this is by focusing on individual users. You should take extraordinary measures not just to acquire users, but also to make them happy."

> "I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy."

**For vision work:** Does the vision describe _how the founder will obsess over the first 10 users_? If the user-experience section of the vision is generic ("we'll provide excellent customer service"), it hasn't internalized this. If it describes the specific lengths the founder is willing to go to ("hand-written notes to every signup, the founder is personally on every customer call for the first year") — that's the Steve Jobs standard, applied at larval scale.

## Understand your users — the one thing if you only had one

From _Startups in 13 Sentences_:

> "Having gotten it down to 13 sentences, I asked myself which I'd choose if I could only keep one. **Understand your users.** That's the key. The essential task in a startup is to create wealth; the dimension of wealth you have most control over is how much you improve users' lives; and the hardest part of that is knowing what to make for them."

> "Understanding your users is part of half the principles in this list. That's the reason to launch early, to understand your users. Evolving your idea is the embodiment of understanding your users... when everything else is collapsing around you, having just ten users who love you will keep you going."

**For vision work:** Does the vision name 10 real users by name (or by tightly-described archetype, like "Alex, a solo accountant who files 80 returns every spring")? If not, the vision is too abstract for survival.

## The Top Idea in Your Mind

From _The Top Idea in Your Mind_ (2010):

> "What one thinks about in the shower in the morning is more important than I'd thought... I think most people have one top idea in their mind at any given time. That's the idea their thoughts will drift toward when they're allowed to drift freely. And this idea will thus tend to get all the benefit of that type of thinking, while others are starved of it. Which means it's a disaster to let the wrong idea become the top one in your mind."

> "I've found there are two types of thoughts especially worth avoiding... thoughts about money [and] disputes. These too are engaging in the wrong way: they have the same velcro-like shape as genuinely interesting ideas, but without the substance."

**For vision work:** This is the deep diagnostic for whether a vision will survive. Is the founder's _top idea_ in the shower really this problem? Or is it fundraising? Or is it some dispute with a former cofounder? The vision can be perfect; if the founder's mind is hijacked by something else, the company won't get built.

**The question to ask:** _What's the first thing you think about in the shower these days? Is it this problem, or something else?_ If something else, name what's stealing the top slot, and figure out how to clear it.

## What this means for the vision factory

When you stress-test a vision for _survival_, run these filters:

| Filter                  | Pass signal                                         | Fail signal                                    |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Growth-as-compass       | Founder names a growth rate they're optimizing for  | "We have ten KPIs"                             |
| Default-alive math      | Founder knows the trajectory without new money      | Implicit assumption that funding will continue |
| Ramen path              | Plausible route to covering founder living costs    | $50M required before anyone earns a salary     |
| Unscalable work         | Founder names specific, embarrassing manual work    | "We'll launch and grow virally"                |
| Insanely-great standard | Founder describes obsessive user attention          | Generic "great UX"                             |
| Ten-users-by-name       | The first cohort is specific people / archetypes    | "Small businesses" / "consumers"               |
| Top-idea check          | This problem occupies the founder's shower-thinking | Founder is mentally elsewhere                  |

A vision that passes ideas, founder, and ambition filters but fails survival filters is a beautiful slide deck that won't ship a product.
